##### Constraining Modified Friction Effects in Gravitational Wave Propagation Using Precessing Binary Black Hole Mergers

&emsp;&emsp;A large class of modified gravity theories predicts that gravitational waves experience friction-like effects during propagation, which alter their amplitude attenuation and further modify the standard gravitational wave luminosity distance. Consequently, by simultaneously measuring the luminosity distance and the source redshift, one can place constraints on such effects. This study investigates the prospects of testing modified friction effects using precessing binary black hole systems observed by ground-based gravitational wave detectors. We simulate 20 precessing events observed by a detector network consisting of two LIGO detectors and two third-generation facilities—the Einstein Telescope and the Cosmic Explorer—and identify the host galaxies through the GLADE+ catalog to obtain redshift information. The results demonstrate that binary black hole precession can significantly improve the measurement accuracy of the luminosity distance, thereby imposing tighter constraints on the modified friction parameters. Under a narrow cosmological prior consistent with the Planck 2018 results, we find that the constraint precision of third-generation gravitational wave detectors on friction effects can far exceed the current results from GWTC-3. This study indicates that precessing binary black holes serve as a powerful tool for testing modified gravity theories and lays the foundation for future relevant research using next-generation gravitational wave observation facilities.
